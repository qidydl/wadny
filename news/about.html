What is this page for?

The news page on wadny.com is more than just a list of entries—it’s nearly a full-featured weblog. Thus, I feel I should explain a few of its features and capabilites, to make it easier to use.

The archives

Every news post ever made with the current system—going back to September 2000—is available through the archives system. You start by clicking on the Archives link in the sidebar. From there, you can select a year, then a month in that year, then a day in that month, and then finally a specific news post. There are no categories (and probably never will be), but hopefully you can find what you’re looking for. If not, you can try your favorite search engine with “site:wadny.com”. Google can be slow to update sometimes, but anything new enough to not be in Google is probably on or near the front page.

The calendar

Another way to get at recent posts is the calendar in the sidebar. It should be fairly straightforward: click on the name of the year to go to the monthly archives, click on the name of the month to get to the daily archives, click on a specific day to go to that day’s archives. The current day (according to EST) is highlighted in white.

Syndication

In addition to the front page and archives, the 10 most recent posts on my site are also available through two different RSS feeds. There are many programs available for viewing RSS feeds; they usually allow you to add a list of feeds and have the program check at regular intervals to see which ones have updated. Both of mine are in RSS 2.0; one has full post text with HTML, and the other has only summaries with no HTML but has an XML stylesheet so if you view it in a web browser, it looks just like a regular page. Both of my feeds should validate.

Comments

My news system also includes the ability for readers to post comments attached to a specific news post. There is no registration required and you can lie all you want, but I reserve the right to edit comments however I see fit, including deleting them entirely. Also, your IP address is logged when you post a comment (primarily for the purpose of banning spammers). It cannot be accessed through the site, so it’s safe from other people, and I promise I won’t do anything stupid with it—I have no reason to chase away any tiny number of readers that may I have. In addition, comments are automatically sanitized to keep my page valid. Non-ASCII characters are transformed into numerical character entities automatically. All XHTML tags are stripped except for <a>, <abbr>, <acronym>, <big>, <code>, <em>, <small>, <strong>, <sub>, and <sup>. URLs are similarly sanitized, but they don’t have to actually be valid URLs—if they 404, I don’t care. Note that you can put any type of URL you want in the field, and if you put in an e-mail address, the mailto: is automatically prepended.

TrackBacks

Late in August 2002, MovableType introduced a system called TrackBack that basically lets you tell the author of a TrackBack-enabled website when you’ve made a post that might interest them. It works by giving each post on a site a unique TrackBack URL that accepts TrackBack “pings.” You can then send a TrackBack ping (basically a simple HTTP POST message) to that URL and it gets sent to the author or recorded and displayed on the page or whatever that particular weblog system does with TrackBack pings. My site displays any TrackBacks after comments on the single-post page. Please note that, as with comments, the IP address of whoever submitted the TrackBack ping is logged, in case spammers start going after TrackBack as well, and I reserve the right to edit TrackBacks however I see fit, including deleting them entirely.

Summary

My news system does a lot of different things, and it takes quite a bit of code to run, but it shouldn’t be too hard to use. If you think anything is named confusingly or could work better, post a comment or send me an e-mail (there’s a link in the sidebar) and I’ll see what I can do.
